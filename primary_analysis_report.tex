\author{}

\begin{abstract}
\noindent We study the effects of AI-based interventions on perceptions of bias in news coverage of China using an online experiment ($N=670$; US and UK). Participants read a news article and were randomly assigned to one of three treatments varying in interactivity: an interactive AI chatbot, a tailored AI-generated comment, or a generic static message. Taking the static message as the baseline, the chatbot significantly increases perceived bias against China by approximately 8.9 points on a 0--100 scale ($p<0.001$). The comment produces a smaller, statistically insignificant effect ($+3.6$ points, $p=0.150$). No significant treatment differences are found for perceived trustworthiness, one-sidedness, or sharing intentions. Exploratory analysis of post-treatment information-processing measures suggests that the desire to verify the article further is positively associated with increased bias perceptions.
\end{abstract}

%============================================================
\section{Experimental Design}
%============================================================

\subsection{Overview}

The experiment studies how AI-generated interventions of varying interactivity affect participants' perceptions of a news article about China. All participants read the same article (``China's Long-Promised Consumer Boom...'') and were randomly assigned to one of three treatment arms:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Static} ($n=184$, 27.5\%): A generic static message. This is the baseline condition.
    \item \textbf{Comment} ($n=215$, 32.1\%): A single, tailored AI-generated comment personalized to the participant's stated opinion.
    \item \textbf{Chatbot} ($n=271$, 40.5\%): Interactive AI chatbot conversation with up to 3 rounds of dialogue.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Outcomes}

Four outcome measures were elicited both before and after treatment on a 0--100 continuous scale:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Primary outcome}: Perceived bias against China.
    \item \textbf{Secondary outcomes}: Perceived trustworthiness, perceived one-sidedness, and sharing likelihood.
\end{enumerate}
The estimand of interest is the change score (post $-$ pre) for each measure.

\subsection{Estimands}

The \textit{primary estimand} is the intent-to-treat (ITT) effect of the Chatbot versus the Static condition on the change in perceived bias against China, controlling for pre-treatment bias. Secondary estimands include the Comment versus Static contrast and all contrasts on the three secondary outcomes.

\section{Exploratory Analysis: Information Processing}
%============================================================

We examine whether three post-treatment information-processing measures predict perception changes \textit{differently} across treatment arms. The three measures, each on a 1--7 Likert scale, are: (i) \textit{fairness evaluation} (``helped me evaluate whether the article was fair''), (ii) \textit{information novelty} (``the information provided is new to me''), and (iii) \textit{verification motive} (``made me want to verify the article further''). Because these variables are measured after treatment, the results should be interpreted as exploratory within-treatment associations, not as causal mediation.

\subsection{Do Information Processing Channels Differ Across Treatments?}

Table~\ref{tab:within_bias} reports OLS regressions of the change in perceived bias on the three information-processing measures, estimated separately within each treatment arm (all controlling for pre-treatment bias). The verification motive is positively and significantly associated with increased perceived bias in both the Comment arm ($\beta = 4.58$, $p < 0.001$) and the Chatbot arm ($\beta = 2.27$, $p = 0.016$), but not in the Static arm ($\beta = 2.36$, $p = 0.131$). By contrast, the fairness evaluation measure shows a marginal positive association only in the Static arm ($\beta = 2.66$, $p = 0.074$). Information novelty is not significantly associated with bias change in any arm.

This pattern suggests that different information-processing channels may operate in different treatments: the verification motive appears to be the active channel in the interactive and tailored treatments (Chatbot and Comment), while the fairness evaluation channel may play a modest role only in the passive condition.

\input{output/tab_within_bias.tex}

\subsection{All Outcomes}

Table~\ref{tab:within_all} extends the within-treatment analysis to all four outcomes. Several patterns emerge. The verification motive is consistently associated with reduced trust in the Chatbot arm ($\beta = -1.88$, $p = 0.011$) and increased one-sidedness perceptions in the Comment arm ($\beta = 4.01$, $p < 0.001$). Information novelty is negatively associated with sharing likelihood in the Chatbot arm ($\beta = -1.93$, $p = 0.008$), suggesting that participants who found the chatbot's information novel were less inclined to share the article. These heterogeneous patterns are consistent with different modes of information processing being activated by different intervention formats.

The central finding is that an interactive AI chatbot significantly increases perceived bias against China in a news article by approximately 9 points on a 0--100 scale relative to a static message. This result is robust across specifications, confirmed by nonparametric permutation inference, and consistent across the US and UK subsamples. A tailored AI comment produces a smaller, statistically insignificant effect. No treatment arm differentially affects trustworthiness, one-sidedness, or sharing intentions.

We emphasize two important limitations on interpretation. First, the experiment identifies shifts in \textit{perceived} bias, not whether the induced perceptions are accurate or welfare-improving. The chatbot may be helping participants recognize genuine bias or inducing a false perception of bias---the current design cannot distinguish these. Second, the design cannot separate the effect of interactivity from the effect of message content: if the chatbot systematically argued that the article was biased against China, the estimated effect reflects exposure to that argument rather than the interactive format \textit{per se}.
